Foundations01 / 16

A clinician who writes, not a writer who reads about clinicians

Why the clinical training matters to the education, and what I bring to a medical affairs team.

I qualified in medicine, rotated through more than twelve specialties, and spent two years writing educational content and assessment material for a medical education platform. That combination shapes how I write: I know what a physician does with a slide once they leave the room, and I know how quickly an audience disengages from a deck that explains the evidence without explaining the decision.

The work here is built on one clinical question — why peripheral intravenous catheters fail in critically ill patients, and what the evidence says about it — followed deliberately through every deliverable a medical affairs team would need.

What I do

  • Turn primary literature into teaching material that holds up to a specialist audience.
  • Build the narrative spine before the slides, so the deck argues something rather than listing findings.
  • Write for the trainer as well as the trainee, so content travels into local markets intact.
  • Check every claim against source, and keep the source where the next writer can find it.

How a paper becomes a slide

The working process behind every sample here — and the point in it where most educational content goes wrong.

StageWhat happensWhat I am guarding against

The failure point

Most educational content breaks at Narrate. A writer who has retrieved and appraised well will still produce an inert deck if they move straight to slides, because slides reward completeness and an argument requires selection. Writing the prose paragraph first forces the omissions to happen early, where they can be defended, rather than late, where they happen by running out of room.

Education · Slide deck03 / 16

Vascular access failure in the critically ill

A 40-minute deck for intensivists and anaesthetists. One message per slide, stated as a claim, not a heading.

Narrative spine, written before any slide

Thesis paragraph

Peripheral intravenous catheters are the most commonly used invasive device in hospital medicine and the least examined. A substantial proportion fail before the end of the intended dwell, and in the critically ill the consequences are not merely inconvenience: interrupted therapy, repeated cannulation, escalation to central access, avoidable infection risk. The evidence suggests failure is not random. It clusters around insertion technique, device selection relative to vein calibre, securement, and the assumption that a working line will keep working. Each is modifiable. This session argues that PIVC failure should be treated as a measurable, preventable clinical outcome rather than an accepted background rate.

Slide sequence, abridged

01

The device we stopped looking at

Open on frequency, not failure. Establishes the scale of exposure before the problem is named.

02

Failure is common enough to be expected, and treated as if it weren't

Reported rates, with study heterogeneity shown honestly rather than averaged into one figure.

03

What "failure" actually means at the bedside

Phlebitis, infiltration, occlusion, dislodgement — separated, because they have different causes and different fixes.

04

The critically ill patient is not the average patient

Vasopressors, oedema, repeated sampling, prolonged dwell. Why ward data understates the problem.

05

Four modifiable factors, ranked by evidence strength

The argumentative centre. Ranking stated as a judgement, with its basis shown.

06

Catheter-to-vein ratio: the measurement most units don't make

Single concept. One diagram, one number, no bullet list.

07

What the evidence does not yet tell us

Placed before the recommendations, not after. Credibility is built by naming gaps first.

08

Three things to change on Monday

Closes on the decision, not the summary.

Design rule applied throughout

Every slide title is a sentence that can be true or false. "Catheter-to-vein ratio" tells the audience the subject; "Catheter-to-vein ratio: the measurement most units don't make" tells them the claim, and the slide then has something to earn.

Education · Speaker notes04 / 16

Notes for the person standing up, not the person who wrote the deck

Sample notes for slide 05, written so a physician who did not build the deck can deliver it with authority.

Slide 05 — Four modifiable factors, ranked by evidence strength

Time: 6 minutes. Your job on this slide: move the room from "failure happens" to "failure is caused."

Say: Walk the four factors in order. Do not read the ranking — explain why the ranking is what it is. It rests on trial design, not effect size alone, and the audience will test you on that.

Expect to be asked: "Isn't securement just a proxy for nursing workload?" A fair challenge, and the honest answer is that the observational data cannot fully separate the two. Concede it. The argument does not depend on winning that point.

Do not say: that any single factor is the cause. The evidence supports a cluster, and overstating it here costs you the room for the rest of the session.

If you are running short: cut factor four and keep the gaps slide. The gaps slide is doing more work than it looks like it is.

Speaker notes are the deliverable most often treated as an afterthought, and the one that decides whether content survives its second delivery. A note that only restates the slide assumes the original author will always present.

Education · Train-the-Trainer05 / 16

Facilitator guide: preparing a physician to teach this

Extract from a Train-the-Trainer pack, designed so faculty in local markets deliver consistent content in their own clinical idiom.

Session architecture

SegmentDurationFacilitator mode
Case cold open5 minPresent, do not interpret. Let the room reach the problem.
Evidence walk15 minDidactic. The only segment where you hold the floor.
Ranking exercise10 minSmall groups rank the four factors before you show your ranking.
Reconciliation7 minCompare. Disagreement here is the teaching, not an obstacle to it.
Local adaptation3 minAsk what changes in their unit. Do not answer for them.

Faculty preparation checklist

  • Read the four source papers, not the deck. You will be asked something the deck does not contain.
  • Know one limitation of each study well enough to raise it before the audience does.
  • Identify one local practice that contradicts the evidence, and name it without blame.
  • Rehearse the reconciliation. It is the only unscripted segment.
  • Confirm which claims are approved for your market and which are not.
Guidance to faculty on tone

You are not persuading this room that a problem exists — most of them have re-sited a failed cannula this week. You are giving them permission to treat it as a measurable outcome rather than an accepted cost of care. That shift is the entire session. If they leave agreeing with the data but still describing failure as inevitable, the session did not work.

Education · Faculty briefing06 / 16

Briefing document for an external expert

What a KOL receives before agreeing to chair or present — scope, evidence position, and the boundaries of the claim.

Extract — evidence position and boundaries

What the evidence supports. That PIVC failure rates in critical care are substantial, that failure is associated with identifiable insertion and maintenance factors, and that structured insertion practice is associated with lower failure in prospective cohorts.

What the evidence does not support. A causal claim for any single intervention in isolation. A quantified number-needed-to-treat. Any comparative claim between named commercial devices, which falls outside the scope of this programme entirely and outside my remit as writer.

Where you will meet resistance. Colleagues who read the observational literature as confounded by nursing ratios. A legitimate reading. The briefing recommends conceding the point rather than defending it, because the educational objective does not require it.

Your independence. Content is yours to challenge. If you disagree with the ranking on slide 05, say so from the platform. A faculty member who visibly disagrees with one slide is worth more to the credibility of the programme than one who agrees with all of them.

Evidence · Data storytelling07 / 16

Building a narrative from a scatter of findings

The same four studies, told two ways. Only one of them is education.

Findings, listed

Study A reported a failure rate at the upper end of the published range. Study B found an association between catheter gauge and phlebitis. Study C found no benefit from routine replacement. Study D reported improved dwell following a structured insertion bundle.

Four true sentences. No argument. An audience cannot act on this, and will not remember it by Thursday.

Findings, narrated

If failure were random, replacing catheters on a schedule would help. Study C says it does not. If failure were random, the device chosen would not matter. Study B says it does. Study D then shows the shape of the answer: change what happens at insertion, and the line lasts longer. Study A tells us why this is worth the trouble — the failure rate is not a rounding error, it is most of a shift's work.

Same four studies. Now they are a claim, and a claim can be argued with, which is what makes it teachable.

Data storytelling is often taken to mean the visual presentation of numbers. It is closer to the opposite: deciding what the numbers are for, and accepting that a finding which does not serve the argument belongs in the appendix, not the deck.

Evidence · Clinical summary08 / 16

One page, for a physician with four minutes

The format that gets read. Structured so the reader can stop after any paragraph and still have something usable.

Evidence summary — PIVC failure in critical care

Bottom line. A large minority of peripheral intravenous catheters fail before intended removal. In critically ill patients the reported burden is higher and the downstream cost — interrupted therapy, escalation to central access — is greater. Failure is associated with modifiable factors.

What is well established. Failure is common across settings. Phlebitis, infiltration, occlusion and dislodgement are distinct mechanisms with distinct antecedents. Routine time-based replacement has not demonstrated benefit over clinically indicated replacement in the trial evidence.

What is probable. Device calibre relative to vein diameter, insertion technique, and securement quality each contribute. Effect estimates vary by setting and by how failure is defined.

What is uncertain. The independent contribution of each factor, and whether observational associations survive adjustment for nursing workload and case-mix.

What this means for practice. Treat failure as an outcome to be measured. Where a unit does not know its own failure rate, that is the first intervention.

Confidence. Moderate for the established claims, low to moderate for the probable ones. Graded on study design and consistency; the rationale sits with the reference set.

Evidence · Search strategy09 / 16

Retrieval, written so it can be repeated by someone else

A search is a method, not an act. Sample strategy in the structure I use for registered reviews.

MEDLINE via Ovid — sample strategy

1 exp Catheterization, Peripheral/ 2 (peripheral adj2 (catheter* or cannula* or line*)).ti,ab,kw. 3 (PIVC or PVC or "peripheral intravenous").ti,ab,kw. 4 1 or 2 or 3 5 exp Critical Illness/ or exp Intensive Care Units/ 6 (critical* ill* or ICU or "intensive care" or anaesthe* or anesthe*).ti,ab,kw. 7 5 or 6 8 (fail* or complicat* or phlebitis or infiltrat* or extravasat* or occlus* or dislodge*).ti,ab,kw. 9 4 and 7 and 8 10 limit 9 to (english language and humans)

Method notes

  • Controlled vocabulary and free text run in parallel, then combined — neither alone retrieves the full set.
  • Truncation is deliberate and tested: complicat* retrieves noun and adjective forms; occlus* retrieves occlusion and occlusive.
  • Adjacency (adj2) rather than AND inside line 2, because "peripheral" and "catheter" co-occurring in an abstract about central lines is a false positive.
  • Translated line by line for Embase (Emtree), CINAHL (CINAHL Headings) and Cochrane CENTRAL; syntax differs, logic does not.
  • Peer reviewed against a checklist before running, with the number retrieved at each line recorded so the search can be audited.

I currently apply this method on a PROSPERO-registered systematic review, with search terms verified by an academic librarian before execution.

Evidence · Critical appraisal10 / 16

Reading a study the way it will be attacked

Appraisal of a hypothetical prospective cohort, showing the judgements recorded before a finding is allowed into a deck.

DomainAssessmentConsequence for the content
DesignProspective cohort. Appropriate for incidence; cannot establish causation.Findings phrased as association throughout. No causal verb.
PopulationSingle-centre, mixed medical-surgical ICU.Generalisability stated as a limitation on the slide, not in the notes.
Outcome definitionComposite endpoint combining four mechanisms.Cannot support a claim about any single mechanism. The most common misuse of this literature.
ConfoundingAdjusted for age, gauge, dwell. Not for nurse-to-patient ratio.Named explicitly. The unadjusted confounder is the first question a good audience asks.
PrecisionWide confidence interval around the secondary outcome.Secondary outcome excluded. The most quotable number in the paper and the least supportable.
ReportingProtocol registered; outcomes as pre-specified.Raises confidence. Recorded in the library against the citation.
The judgement

Usable for incidence and for the association between insertion factors and composite failure. Not usable for mechanism-specific claims, and not usable for the secondary outcome regardless of how well it would present. A striking number from a wide confidence interval is a liability on a slide a physician will be asked to defend.

Evidence · Data visualisation11 / 16

Working with an analyst: the brief, not the request

What I send a data analyst so the figure that comes back is interpretable by a clinician rather than merely accurate.

Figure brief — failure rate by catheter-to-vein ratio

The single claim this figure must support: that failure rises as the catheter occupies more of the vein, and that the rise is not linear.

Audience: intensivists and anaesthetists, viewing for under eight seconds, on a projected slide at the back of a lecture theatre.

Show: ratio on x, failure proportion on y, confidence intervals. Mark the threshold where the relationship steepens.

Do not show: individual study markers. Four studies plotted as four shapes will be read as four contradictions.

Axis discipline: y-axis begins at zero. If the effect is only visible on a truncated axis, we do not have the effect.

Colour: one hue. Any second colour must encode something. Decorative colour on a clinical figure invites the audience to look for a meaning that isn't there.

What I need back: the underlying values, so the figure can be verified against source before review. A figure I cannot check is a figure I cannot sign off.

The most useful thing a writer can give an analyst is the sentence the figure has to prove. Without it, the analyst optimises for completeness — and completeness is what makes clinical figures unreadable.

Education · Plain language12 / 16

The same evidence, for the patient it happened to

Register control is the writing skill that transfers least automatically. This is the same clinical content as section 08.

Patient-facing summary

A drip — the small tube placed in a vein in your hand or arm — is the most common procedure in hospital. It is also one that often has to be redone. The tube can become blocked, the vein can become sore or swollen, or the tube can come loose.

When this happens, treatment is paused while a new drip is placed. For someone who is seriously unwell, those pauses matter, and repeated attempts are uncomfortable.

Researchers have been looking at why this happens. It appears not to be simply bad luck. How the drip is placed, how well it is secured, and whether the tube is the right size for the vein all seem to make a difference.

What this means for you: it is reasonable to tell your nurse or doctor early if the area around your drip becomes painful, swollen or red. Catching it early is easier than replacing it late.

Written at a reading age appropriate for a general audience, without the two failures that characterise most plain-language writing: retaining the clinical structure while swapping the words, or simplifying so far that the reader is left with no action.

Evidence · Abstract & poster13 / 16

Abstract discipline: 250 words that survive peer review

Structured abstract in congress format, and the layout logic behind the accompanying poster.

Structured abstract — illustrative

Background. Peripheral intravenous catheter failure is common in critical care, but the relative contribution of modifiable insertion factors is unclear.

Methods. Systematic review of prospective studies reporting PIVC failure in adult critical care. Four databases searched from inception; two reviewers screened independently; risk of bias assessed with a validated tool. Heterogeneity precluded meta-analysis; findings synthesised narratively.

Results. Reported failure rates varied substantially, driven largely by inconsistent outcome definitions. Composite failure was consistently associated with catheter-to-vein ratio and securement quality across included studies. No included study adjusted for nursing workload.

Conclusions. Failure is associated with modifiable factors, but the evidence base is limited by definitional heterogeneity. Standardised outcome definitions are a prerequisite for pooled estimates. Units should measure local failure rates before selecting an intervention.

Poster logic

  • The reader is standing, has ninety seconds, and is holding a coffee. The poster has one job: transmit the conclusion.
  • Conclusion sits top-right, at eye height, where the eye lands second. Not at the bottom, where posters conventionally bury it.
  • Methods compressed to a single flow strip. Anyone who needs more will ask, and the presenter is standing there.
  • One figure, legible at two metres. Two figures means neither is read.
Governance · Medical review14 / 16

Reviewing content against standards, and the audit trail that proves it

The checklist run before content goes to medical, scientific and compliance sign-off.

Content review checklist

  • Every claim traced to a named source, and the source says what the claim says.
  • No causal language attached to observational data.
  • No comparative claim about a named product unless within approved scope and supported by head-to-head evidence.
  • Figures reconciled against underlying values, not against the previous version of the figure.
  • Limitations stated in the deck, not only in the speaker notes.
  • Off-label content identified and removed or flagged for the relevant approval route.
  • Reference list complete, formatted consistently, every citation reachable.
  • Version numbered; changes since last approved version listed for the reviewer.
Audit trail principle

The purpose of the trail is not to record that review happened. It is to let a reviewer eighteen months later establish which evidence a specific approved claim rested on, and whether that evidence has since changed. A trail that logs approvals but not the evidence version behind them answers the wrong question.

Governance · Reference library15 / 16

A library built during the work, not after it

Structure for a searchable clinical evidence library that the next writer can actually use.

FieldWhy it exists
Citation, DOI, full-text locationRetrieval without a second search.
Study design, population, settingAnswers "can I use this here?" without reopening the paper.
Outcome definition as stated by the authorsThe single most common source of error in this literature.
Appraisal judgement and its basisSo the next writer inherits the reasoning, not just the verdict.
Claims this source supportsReverse index. Moves a reviewer from claim to evidence in one step.
Claims this source is not sufficient forPrevents the paper being over-cited by someone who did not read it.
Approved-for-market statusKeeps the compliance boundary attached to the evidence.
Superseded-byThe field that keeps a library from quietly going out of date.

The last two rows are the ones usually missing. A reference library without a superseded-by field will, given eighteen months, confidently hand a writer a retracted paper.
